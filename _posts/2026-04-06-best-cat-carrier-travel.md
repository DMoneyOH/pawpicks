## What to Look For

Choosing the ideal carrier means looking beyond aesthetics. Here are the crucial factors we always consider when evaluating cat carriers:

### Material & Durability
Cat carriers come in various materials, each with pros and cons. Soft-sided carriers, often made from nylon or mesh, are lightweight and collapsible, making them easy to store. They're great for casual use, short trips, or when you need to store the carrier away easily. However, they might not offer enough protection for very anxious cats prone to scratching or for air travel where hard-sided options are often mandatory. Hard-sided carriers, usually made from sturdy plastic, provide more protection and are easier to clean. They're excellent for air travel, long car trips, or for cats who are escape artists. Wicker or fabric-covered cardboard carriers exist, but we generally advise against them due to poor ventilation, difficulty cleaning, and lack of durability. Look for robust zippers, strong seams, and chew-resistant materials.

### Size & Comfort
This is paramount. Your cat should be able to stand up, turn around, and lie down comfortably in the carrier. A carrier that's too small will cause stress and discomfort, while one that's too large can allow your cat to be tossed around during movement, increasing anxiety and potential for injury. Measure your cat from nose to tail and from the top of their head to the ground. The carrier should be at least 1.5 times their height and length. Consider adding a soft blanket or a familiar toy inside to make it more inviting. For kittens, remember they grow quickly, so a slightly larger carrier might be a wise investment.

### Security & Safety
A carrier's primary job is to keep your cat safe and secure. Look for strong, reliable latches or zippers that can't be easily opened by a clever paw. Top-loading carriers can be a godsend for cats who resist entering front-opening ones. Ensure there are no sharp edges or pinch points that could injure your cat. If you plan for car travel, check for seatbelt loops or restraints that allow you to secure the carrier, preventing it from sliding around. This is a critical feature for any cat carrier travel scenario. For air travel, always verify that the carrier meets airline-specific dimensions and safety requirements for both in-cabin and cargo transport.

### Ventilation
Good airflow is vital to prevent overheating and reduce anxiety. Look for carriers with ample mesh windows or ventilation holes on multiple sides. This not only ensures a fresh supply of air but also allows your cat to see their surroundings, which can be reassuring for some and stimulating for others. Avoid carriers with minimal openings or those made of solid, non-breathable materials, especially in warmer climates or during long journeys. Proper ventilation helps regulate temperature and keeps the environment inside the carrier more comfortable for your feline friend.

### Ease of Cleaning
Let's face it, accidents happen. Whether it's a nervous pee, a hairball, or a spilled treat, a carrier needs to be easy to clean. Hard-sided carriers often win here, as they can be simply wiped down or hosed off. Soft-sided carriers should ideally have removable, machine-washable liners or be made of water-resistant fabric that can be spot cleaned. Look for carriers with minimal crevices where messes can hide and fester. A clean carrier is a hygienic carrier, and one that doesn't smell like past incidents will be more appealing to your cat for future trips.

### Portability & Storage
Think about how you'll be transporting the carrier. Does it have comfortable handles? Are there wheels for longer distances? If you'll be carrying your cat for extended periods, a shoulder strap can be incredibly helpful. For those with limited space, a collapsible soft-sided carrier or a hard-sided carrier that disassembles for storage can be a real space-saver. Consider the carrier's weight, especially if you have a larger cat; a heavy carrier plus a heavy cat can be quite a burden.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even with the best intentions, new pet owners (and even experienced ones!) can sometimes make missteps when it comes to cat carriers. Here are a few common pitfalls to steer clear of:

### Only Using the Carrier for Vet Visits
If the only time your cat sees their carrier is when they're being taken to the vet for shots or an unpleasant procedure, they're going to associate it with negative experiences. This makes every future trip a battle. We recommend leaving the carrier out in your home with the door open, adding a comfy blanket or some treats inside. Let your cat explore it on their own terms, turning it into a safe, familiar den rather than a symbol of stress.

### Not Acclimating Your Cat to the Carrier
Simply buying a great carrier isn't enough; you need to introduce it properly. Start by making it a positive space. Place treats, toys, or catnip inside. Feed your cat near or even inside the carrier. Gradually close the door for short periods while your cat is inside, then open it. Slowly increase the time, eventually lifting the carrier and walking a short distance. This gradual introduction is key for a calm cat carrier travel experience.

### Overlooking Proper Restraints in the Car
Many owners simply place the carrier on the seat or floor without securing it. In the event of a sudden stop or accident, an unsecured carrier can become a dangerous projectile, risking injury to both your cat and human passengers. Always use seatbelts, bungee cords, or dedicated car seat tethers to secure the carrier firmly in place. Safety first, always!

### Forgetting Essentials for Longer Trips
For trips longer than an hour or two, your cat will need more than just a carrier. Think about hydration and hunger. Pack a small dish and some water, and consider a portable litter box if it's a very long journey. For extended stays, we often recommend bringing along one of the [automatic cat feeders for travel](/pawpicks/pet-accessories/best-automatic_cat_feeder/) to maintain their feeding schedule and reduce stress in an unfamiliar environment. Don't forget their favorite toy or a comfort blanket.

## FAQ

### Q: My cat hates their carrier. How can I make it less stressful?
A: Start by making the carrier a positive space. Leave it open with treats and toys inside. Feed your cat near or in it. Use pheromone sprays like Feliway 30 minutes before travel, and cover the carrier with a light blanket to create a den-like feel.

### Q: Can my cat go in the plane cabin with me?
A: Many airlines allow small cats in the cabin if they fit under the seat in an approved carrier. Always check your specific airline's pet policy, size restrictions, and fees well in advance. Soft-sided carriers like the [Sherpa Original Deluxe Travel Pet Carrier](https://amzn.to/4sVt7G2) are often ideal for this.

### Q: What's the best way to clean a cat carrier after an accident?
A: For hard-sided carriers, use an enzymatic cleaner to break down odors and stains, then wipe down with soap and water. For soft-sided carriers, remove and machine wash any liners, then spot clean the fabric with a pet-safe cleaner. Air dry thoroughly.

### Q: Should I put food and water in the carrier for short trips?
A: For short trips (under 2-3 hours), generally no, as it can spill and create a mess. For longer trips, offer water during stops. For very long journeys, a small, spill-proof dish can be attached, but monitor for spills.

### Q: Hard-sided or soft-sided – which is better?
A: It depends on your needs. Hard-sided offers more protection and is easier to clean, often required for cargo air travel. Soft-sided is lighter, collapsible, and often more comfortable for in-cabin air travel. Consider your cat's temperament and your primary use.
